SHORT ESSAY, BIG CHALLENGE

650 words. The essay is the one part of this reductive process where you get to show up and be seen as more than just numbers, stats and lists. The admissions officers actually want to know you (really!), but you only have 650 words. From initial brainstorming through final polish, I'll guide you with thought-provoking questions and honest, productive feedback, as you write a personal statement that is your best work: authentic, compelling, and memorable.

THE A-HA! MOMENT

"But I'm just like every other science nerd"

"I don't have a hook, I like all my classes the same"

"My grades aren't great, that's all they'll notice."

The struggle is real. A lot of students have similar backgrounds, experiences, and interests, and many can't think of ways to present themselves that stand out. That's why I dig, talk, question and explore – because there's nothing like that "a-ha" moment when the student and I finally lock on to the topic, the story that makes them come alive on the page as an individual. So often the best stories pop up in a throwaway comment or random aside that comes up while we're talking about something else:

"…since I can't be a Bollywood princess I might as well…"

–"…it's like that time when I quit ballet…"

–"…I could always paint another mural in the garage…"

Wait, what?! Tell me more…! Each of those random comments led to a stellar and successful essay.

SUCCESS STORIES

The Bollywood Fan

▼

The Candidate: this STEM-focused student loved Indian musicals so much that she made regular pilgrimages from Los Feliz to Indian-language theaters in Artesia!

The Essay: this story showed her quirky and artsy side along with major passion and determination.

The Outcome: she was accepted to every school she applied to and wound up at Brown.

The Ex-Ballet Dancer

▼

The Candidate: prior to quitting ballet, the student was on a professional track, taking serious dance classes five days a week. Additionally, her grandmother had been a dancer and her mother had trained seriously.

The Essay: it focused on how, as a middle-schooler, she had to make a life-defining choice with huge implications for her future, her role in her family, and her own self-image. It demonstrated real maturity, self-awareness and gave her a chance to mention the remarkable discipline she gained from her dance training – part of her character which wasn't evident anywhere else in her application.

The Outcome: she applied early to her first choice, Barnard, and was accepted.

The Muralist

▼

The Candidate: this student was academically unexceptional, and had few interests beyond her boyfriend and her social life. She didn't have a "hook," and we really struggled to find a topic for her.

The Essay: grounded at home and bored, the student painted a mural that spanned a wall in the garage. The story let her present herself as both creative and a do-er – someone capable of setting a big goal for herself and achieving it.

The Outcome: she got into Emerson off the wait-list. An admissions officer told her parents "I couldn't stop thinking about that mural!"

The Kicker: After we finished the essay, I finally saw the mural… and it was terrible! But it didn't matter because this student was not applying with an artistic portfolio, and the heart of the story was 100% true.

HOW I GOT HERE

I'm a product of the New York City private school world and a graduate of Wesleyan University, so the ecosystem of elite admissions has long been familiar to me. I started working in the office of test prep giant The Princeton Review when I was a senior in high school. After college, while growing a career in the entertainment industry, I consistently worked on the side as a tutor for the SAT, GMAT & LSAT.

Because it's more personal and creative than test prep, my favorite part was always when I'd continue to work with my standardized test students on their essays and applications. Even after I stopped tutoring, friends with kids would reach out for essay help, all too aware that no matter how smart and accomplished they might be, it's utterly impossible for a parent to help with this task. That's where the Auntie part comes in. I love digging in deep with my essay students and getting to know them through this exciting-yet-vulnerable, life-changing period. In fact, I consider it a privilege.

My story-telling chops are top-notch. I'm an award-winning writer/producer who did years in the trenches of the movie trailer business before pivoting to non-fiction television. Most recently I've been working on Discovery Channel's Expedition Unknown for six seasons and have filmed in over 20 countries. Helping students find their stories remains my favorite creative work.
